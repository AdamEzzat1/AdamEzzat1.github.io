TL;DR

ABNG (Adaptive Belief Network Graph) is an experimental architecture that treats belief states as a first-class object: each node in an adaptive trie carries its own Bayesian Linear Regression posterior, routing is a traceable walk through learned bytes, the graph can grow / split / merge / prune / compress / freeze under a deterministic policy, and every state mutation appends to a SHA-256 hash-chained audit log so the training history is replayable bit-for-bit. This piece walks through what the architecture is, the neighbourhood it sits in, how it works internally, what it might be good for (with caveats), what could go wrong, where it sits in the literature, and what experiments would falsify it. ABNG is exploratory engineering, not validated science. No published benchmarks yet — the closer names three specific experiments whose outcomes would change the project’s direction.

Two prototype disclaimers up front

ABNG is a research-stage prototype, not a product. It has unit tests, property tests, fuzz coverage, a working CLI, and zero published benchmarks. It is also built on top of CJC-Lang, a research compiler that is itself still pre-1.0 — twenty-one Rust crates, roughly 96K lines of code, an actively evolving language definition. Anything ABNG inherits from the substrate inherits the substrate’s caveats. This piece is a design exploration meant for scrutiny, not a launch.

Where the cracks show

There’s a recurring discomfort with most modern neural networks: they are confidently wrong without telling you, their decisions are hard to audit after the fact, and their compute graphs are fixed at design time even when the data isn’t. We have partial fixes for each — Bayesian layers, attention visualizations, mixture-of-experts, conformal wrappers — but they tend to be bolted onto architectures that did not begin with these properties in mind.

This article describes ABNG (Adaptive Belief Network Graph), an experimental architecture that tries the opposite: design from those constraints — posterior belief at every node, replayable history, structural adaptation — and see what falls out.

If you came looking for a “transformer killer,” this isn’t that. It’s a sketch of a different shape of computation, with all the unfinished edges that implies.

Modern architectures produce accurate point estimates, but they share a small set of recurring discomforts.

Epistemic opacity. A model returns a number, and you have no principled way to know whether it is confident, guessing, or extrapolating beyond the data it saw at training time. Calibrated uncertainty exists as a layer you bolt on — temperature scaling, MC dropout, deep ensembles, conformal prediction — not as a property of the architecture itself.

Auditability. When a model trained over months produces a strange output, the lineage of that decision — what data shaped it, which gradient steps mattered, what part of the network “owns” the answer — is essentially unrecoverable. For ad ranking this is annoying. For scientific simulations, regulated decisions, or any system whose outputs must be defended after the fact, it is a real problem.

Structural rigidity. Once you fix a transformer’s depth, width, and head count, the compute graph is set. Mixture-of-Experts adds conditional routing, but the expert population is still fixed. The model does not grow, prune, or specialize in response to evidence; the engineer does, by retraining.

Reproducibility tax. Non-determinism creeps in through floating-point reductions, GPU kernel ordering, parallel scheduling, and stochastic optimizers. Two “identical” training runs produce two different artifacts. Tolerable for research; painful for any system that must be replayed, audited, or proven correct.

None of these are catastrophic alone. Together, they constrain where you can use modern ML responsibly — and they have driven a small zoo of partial fixes: Bayesian neural networks, probabilistic graphical models, neural state-space models, liquid networks, conformal prediction, active inference. Each addresses one or two pieces of the problem.

ABNG is an attempt to take a coherent run at several of them at once, by treating the model itself as an evolving belief state and demanding bit-level determinism from the engineering substrate.

Belief states, used precisely

“Belief state” gets used loosely. In a Bayesian sense it is a probability distribution over unknowns, updated as evidence arrives. In control and reinforcement learning it is a sufficient statistic for prediction. In a probabilistic graphical model it is the posterior over hidden variables.

ABNG takes the Bayesian view and pushes it into the architecture. Each node in an adaptive graph carries a small per-node MLP that produces features, with a Bayesian Linear Regression (BLR) head on top — a Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior over (weights, noise variance) that is updated in closed form as data arrives, with no variational machinery required. Routing uses the same Node4 / Node16 / Node48 / Node256 child layout as Adaptive Radix Trees, holding belief-graph children indexed by quantile-encoded feature bytes rather than database keys.

As observations flow through the graph:

  • Routing is a traceable walk through learned bytes, not an opaque attention pattern.
  • Each leaf separately reports epistemic uncertainty (the model’s uncertainty about its own parameters, from the BLR posterior) and aleatoric uncertainty (irreducible noise in the data, from the inverse-gamma component).
  • The graph itself can grow, split, merge, prune, compress, or freeze nodes — each action a deterministic function of per-node statistics (15-bin ECE calibration, density-based out-of-distribution score, drift baseline, route entropy) under an installed DecisionPolicy.
  • Every state mutation is appended to a SHA-256 hash-chained audit log. The model at any moment in its history can be replayed bit-for-bit from the log.

That last property is the one most worth dwelling on. Given the same seed and the same observation sequence, two ABNG runs produce a byte-identical snapshot. Not “approximately the same loss curve” — the same bytes, across re-runs, currently verified on x86-64 Linux, macOS, and Windows (ARM is on the roadmap but untested at the time of writing). This is a deliberate, expensive constraint inherited from CJC-Lang: Kahan-compensated summation everywhere, no fused multiply-add in any kernel that touches belief state, SplitMix64 RNG threaded explicitly, BTreeMap throughout, no HashMap in canonical paths.

You should raise an eyebrow at this. Bit-identical determinism in deep learning is hard. ABNG can claim it partly because the per-node architecture is small (an MLP+BLR head per node, not a 100-billion-parameter transformer), and partly because CJC-Lang has been engineered around the constraint from the start. None of that means it scales. That is one of the more honest open questions in the project, and the critical-analysis section (coming in the follow-up) treats it bluntly.

Why uncertainty and auditability are worth the cost

If you already see calibrated uncertainty and audit trails as desirable, skip ahead. Otherwise, three pressures push them past “nice to have”:

Scientific machine learning. Physics-informed networks, surrogate solvers, and inverse problems live or die on calibrated uncertainty. A surrogate that is confidently wrong outside its training regime can silently corrupt a simulation. ABNG is partly an answer to “what if every prediction came with a defensible posterior, not a point estimate?”

Regulated and safety-critical ML. When a decision must be defended — to a regulator, a court, a clinician, or a postmortem — “why this output?” needs a paper trail. Replayability is the strongest possible form of paper trail: you can re-derive the model from the audit log and verify nothing has been tampered with.

Debugging at the architectural level. Anyone who has chased a non-determinism bug across GPUs knows the cost. A reproducible substrate does not make ML easier, but it makes the bugs you do hit actually debuggable, because failures are the same every time.

Each motivation has a cost. Uncertainty propagation is compute. Audit logs are storage. Deterministic kernels are slower than vendor-tuned BLAS. The argument is not that ABNG’s design is universally better — it is that there is a specific class of problems where these tradeoffs invert, and current architectures do not serve that class well.

Where ABNG sits on the map

Before getting into ABNG’s specifics, it helps to know the neighbourhood. The architectures below are the ones whose ideas ABNG either borrows from, competes with, or is most likely to be confused with. For each I give the core idea in one paragraph, then strengths, weaknesses, and how it behaves along four axes that matter most for this article: scaling, interpretability, memory handling, and uncertainty handling.

Transformers

Core idea. Compute a weighted combination of every token against every other token via dot-product attention, repeated across many layers. The architecture is parameter-heavy, embarrassingly parallel, and structurally identical across an entire training run.

Dimension Behaviour
Strengths Best-in-class on language, vision, code. Massively parallel. Empirically scales smoothly with parameters and data.
Weaknesses Attention is O(n²) in sequence length. Attention maps are post-hoc, not principled explanations. No built-in uncertainty. Fixed compute graph.
Scaling Excellent in width and depth; sequence length is the bottleneck. Long context costs grow superlinearly without engineering tricks (FlashAttention, sliding windows, sparse attention).
Interpretability Limited. Attention weights are increasingly understood to be unreliable as faithful explanations of model behaviour.
Memory handling KV cache grows linearly with context. There is no learned memory state — memory is whatever fits in the context window.
Uncertainty handling None native. Calibration must be added with temperature scaling, deep ensembles, MC dropout, or conformal wrappers.

State-Space Models (Mamba, S4, S5)

Core idea. Replace attention with a discretized linear (or input-conditioned) state-space recurrence — a structured ODE — that compresses a sequence into a fixed-size hidden state and updates it as new tokens arrive. The Mamba family added selective state, where transition matrices depend on the input, recovering much of attention’s expressivity at linear cost.

Dimension Behaviour
Strengths Linear scaling in sequence length. Fast inference, especially streaming. Hidden state is bounded — closer to a recurrent computer’s working memory than a transformer’s KV cache.
Weaknesses Less proven on knowledge-heavy benchmarks; recent results are competitive but not yet a sweep. Structured state imposes assumptions that may or may not fit the task. Training stability requires care.
Scaling Linear in sequence length. Reasonable scaling in width and depth, though the parameter-per-FLOP curve looks different from transformers.
Interpretability In principle, the hidden state has interpretable dynamics. In practice, the state vectors are still high-dimensional and not human-readable.
Memory handling Fixed-size hidden state. Memory is learned compression of history, not raw retention.
Uncertainty handling None native.

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

Core idea. Pass messages along the edges of an explicit graph; each node aggregates messages from its neighbours and updates its feature vector. Topology is given as part of the input, not learned.

Dimension Behaviour
Strengths Encodes relational structure natively. Strong on molecules, citation networks, traffic, knowledge graphs. Sparse computation if the graph is sparse.
Weaknesses Over-smoothing with depth (node features collapse to similar vectors). Expressivity is bounded by Weisfeiler-Lehman tests in vanilla forms. Topology is fixed per input.
Scaling Excellent on sparse graphs; quadratic-ish on dense ones. Depth is limited by over-smoothing more than by compute.
Interpretability Reasonable. Per-node and per-edge features are inspectable; attention or attribution methods often work well.
Memory handling Linear in graph size; embeddings stored per node.
Uncertainty handling None native, but Bayesian GNN variants (variational, MCMC, ensemble) are an active research area.

Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs)

Core idea. Replace point-estimate weights with full posterior distributions, typically approximated by variational families, MCMC samples, or deep ensembles. Predictions become predictive distributions instead of single numbers.

Dimension Behaviour
Strengths Principled uncertainty by construction. Integration with decision theory is clean. Often regularizes well under data scarcity.
Weaknesses Variational posteriors are usually mean-field Gaussian, which is a known poor match for actual posterior geometry. MCMC doesn’t scale to deep networks. Ensembles are expensive at both train and inference time.
Scaling Significantly more expensive per parameter than deterministic networks. Memory is 2–3× for the cheapest mean-field variants.
Interpretability Weight posteriors are conceptually inspectable; in practice they’re opaque in any nontrivial network.
Memory handling No special memory mechanism beyond the underlying architecture.
Uncertainty handling The defining feature. Epistemic and aleatoric can be decomposed, given the right formulation.

Liquid Neural Networks

Core idea. Continuous-time dynamics: each neuron’s state evolves according to an ODE whose parameters depend on inputs. Liquid Time-Constant (LTC) networks and the related Liquid-S4 family aim for very small parameter counts, online adaptability, and dynamics that don’t decay to a fixed equilibrium.

Dimension Behaviour
Strengths Compact parameter counts. Strong on small robotics and time-series tasks (the famous “19-neuron lane-keeping” result from Hasani et al.). Theoretically tractable dynamics in the small.
Weaknesses Scaling to large modern workloads is unproven. Training stability for stiff dynamics is a known headache. Less mature tooling.
Scaling Best at small-to-medium scale. Open question at LLM scale.
Interpretability Continuous dynamics are inspectable in low dimensions; phase plots, attractors, and Lyapunov analysis are available. Less useful at high dimensions.
Memory handling Continuous-time state implicitly retains history; bounded by the ODE’s contraction properties.
Uncertainty handling None native.

Neural Operators (FNO, DeepONet, GNO)

Core idea. Learn mappings between function spaces rather than between finite-dimensional vectors. Fourier Neural Operators apply learned filters in the spectral domain; DeepONet factors operators into a branch-trunk architecture; Graph Neural Operators discretize on irregular meshes.

Dimension Behaviour
Strengths Discretization-invariant: train on one mesh resolution, evaluate on another. Strong on PDE surrogates, inverse problems, climate modelling, fluid dynamics.
Weaknesses Specialized to operator-learning tasks. Not general-purpose. Spectral methods have known issues at sharp discontinuities (Gibbs phenomena).
Scaling Reasonable in spatial dimensions; FFT-based variants scale as O(n log n).
Interpretability The operator structure is mathematically explicit (spectral coefficients, kernel decompositions).
Memory handling Bounded by spectral truncation or basis choice.
Uncertainty handling None native; Bayesian extensions (e.g. probabilistic FNOs) are an active research area.

Where ABNG sits among them

ABNG is best understood as a deliberate recombination, not a new family. Three lineages meet in the design:

  1. Graph Neural Networks give it the per-node feature-vector mental model and the message-passing intuition. But ABNG’s graph is a learned routing trie, not an input-supplied topology, and it changes shape during training under the structural decision policy.

  2. Bayesian Neural Networks give it the calibrated-uncertainty target. ABNG localizes the Bayesian machinery: instead of a global weight posterior, each leaf node carries a small Bayesian Linear Regression head with a Normal-Inverse-Gamma prior, updated in closed form. Strictly speaking this places ABNG closer to a routed mixture of Bayesian Linear Regression experts — what the older literature on Bayesian committee machines and products of local experts (Tresp 2000; Cao & Fleet 2014) was reaching for — than to a full Bayesian Neural Network. The uncertainty estimate is local, conditional on routing, not a posterior over the whole model.

  3. Mixture-of-Experts gives it the conditional-compute intuition: only the routed leaf produces a prediction, and only that leaf’s posterior is updated. But MoE routers learn a soft gating function and the expert population is fixed; ABNG’s router walks a discrete, deterministic trie over quantile-encoded features, and the population of experts (leaves) is itself adaptive — Grow, Split, Merge, Prune, Compress, Freeze. (A reader who tracks Adaptive Computation Time, Pondernet, or Universal Transformers will recognize the dynamic-compute family resemblance, but ABNG adapts at the training-graph level rather than the per-token-inference level.)

ABNG combines these lineages with two further commitments that are uncommon in any of them:

  • A SHA-256 audit chain over every state mutation, making the entire training history replayable from a serialized log. This is closer to a database (write-ahead log, Merkle chain) than to a deep learning system.
  • A determinism contract enforced from the ground up by CJC-Lang. Kahan summation, no FMA in belief-touching kernels, deterministic dispatch, BTreeMap throughout. Two ABNG runs with the same seed produce a byte-identical snapshot — a property that nothing in the comparison table above offers.
What Makes This Different?

Most modern architectures pick one axis: transformers go all-in on scale, SSMs on sequence-length efficiency, GNNs on relational structure, BNNs on uncertainty, Liquid NNs on compactness, Neural Operators on function-space learning. ABNG instead picks three secondary axes that are usually treated as bolt-ons — calibrated belief, structural adaptation, and bit-identical reproducibility — and makes them load-bearing. The cost is that ABNG is not yet competitive with the headliners on their own axes (it has no answer to GPT-scale parameter counts, no demonstrated long-context advantage, no general-purpose benchmark sweep). The hypothesis is that the combination matters more than topping any single chart.

Caveats on this comparison

A few claims here are reasonable extrapolation rather than established research, and they deserve explicit flags:

  • “Bayesian GNN variants are an active research area” is true, but the work is fragmented; there is no consensus best practice analogous to “use ensembles for BNN uncertainty.”
  • ABNG’s per-node BLR produces a clean epistemic/aleatoric split at the local linear model: the middle component of the predictor (φᵀΛ⁻¹φ) is a dimensionless leverage term, and the aleatoric scale is b/(a-1) from the inverse-gamma; predictive variance combines them. Whether the graph-level prediction inherits a clean decomposition depends on whether routing uncertainty is also modelled, which it is in ABNG’s composite out-of-distribution score but not in a fully principled posterior-over-routes way.
  • “Bit-identical determinism” is currently demonstrated on x86-64 Linux / macOS / Windows in CJC-Lang’s test suite. ARM, GPU offload, and distributed training are out of scope at present.

How the architecture actually works

This section walks through ABNG’s mechanisms from the inside out, starting with the simplest case (a single observation enters the graph) and building up to the structural decisions that reshape the graph during training. Everything described here corresponds to running code in crates/cjc-abng/ at Phase 0.7. Some details (snapshot version v13, the 30 frozen audit-kind tags 0x00..0x1D, the six structural action types, the 14-threshold DecisionPolicy) are stable contracts; others will evolve as Phase 0.8 lands. Where a design choice is provisional, I flag it.

The one-sentence mental model

ABNG is a routed mixture of Bayesian experts arranged as a trie, where the trie itself adapts during training under a deterministic policy, and every state change is recorded in a hash-chained audit log.

Each clause maps to a mechanism. The rest of the section unpacks them in order.

From feature vector to routed leaf

The first job is to turn a real-valued input into a deterministic walk through the graph.

ABNG installs a QuantileCodebook once, before any node is added. For each input dimension d, the codebook holds a sorted list of bin boundaries — empirically chosen quantiles of that dimension over a calibration set. At inference time, each feature value is bucketized into one of n_bins ∈ {2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256} bins, producing a single byte per dimension. The result is a fixed-length route key — a sequence of bytes derived deterministically from the input.

Why a discrete byte route instead of a learned soft router? Two reasons. First, it makes the routing path traceable: you can write the exact byte sequence into an audit event and replay the descent later. Second, the byte representation aligns naturally with the Adaptive Radix Tree (ART) children layout that ABNG uses for its graph topology.

The graph itself is a trie. Each node carries an AdaptiveChildren payload that takes one of five forms — None, Node4, Node16, Node48, Node256 — and auto-promotes to the next size when full. This is mechanically identical to ART’s well-known children layout; ABNG borrows the structure and replaces the database-key payload with belief-graph children indexed by route-key bytes.

Descent itself is a one-pass loop. In pseudo-code:

def descend(graph, route_key):
    node = graph.root
    matched_prefix = []
    for byte in route_key:
        child = node.children.get(byte)
        if child is None:
            break  # longest-prefix match; stop here
        matched_prefix.append(byte)
        node = child
    return RouteEvidence(leaf=node, matched_prefix=matched_prefix)

If the route key runs out of bound children before reaching a configured depth, descent stops at the deepest matching node — longest prefix wins. If Routed audit events are enabled (opt-in, off by default to avoid log explosion), the matched prefix and final leaf id are recorded as a 0x1B Routed event.

The MLP body. Each leaf has its own small MLP, initialized deterministically: weights are sampled from a Xavier-uniform distribution whose seed is derived as mix(graph.seed, node_id, layer_idx, kind_bit) using SplitMix64. Biases start at zero. The MLP feeds into the BLR head; you can think of it as a learned feature extractor specialized to this leaf’s slice of input space.

The BLR head. The BLR is where the Bayesian machinery actually lives. ABNG uses the Normal-Inverse-Gamma conjugate model for Gaussian-noise linear regression: the prior over weights is N(m, σ²Λ⁻¹) and the prior over noise variance is IG(a, b). Given a new data point (φ, y) — where φ is the MLP feature vector — the update is closed-form:

\[ \Lambda_{\text{new}} = \Lambda + \phi\phi^\top, \quad m_{\text{new}} = \Lambda_{\text{new}}^{-1}(\Lambda m + \phi y), \]

\[ a_{\text{new}} = a + \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad b_{\text{new}} = b + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(y^2 + m^\top \Lambda m - m_{\text{new}}^\top \Lambda_{\text{new}} m_{\text{new}}\right). \]

This is textbook conjugate Bayesian linear regression (Bishop 2006 §3.3; Murphy 2012 §7.6) — established research, no novelty claim. The interesting bit is that ABNG implements it in determinism-preserving Rust: hand-rolled Cholesky and triangular-solve (~30 LOC, Kahan-compensated, no FMA), BLR.state_hash() exposed for audit verification, and a feature_version_hash stamped at init so a stale-feature update is rejected with a typed error instead of silently corrupting the posterior.

Predictions from the BLR return three numbers:

def blr_predict(blr, phi):
    mean = blr.m @ phi
    # solve Λ x = φ once, reuse for leverage and predictive variance
    x = cholesky_solve(blr.L, phi)
    epistemic_leverage = phi @ x          # dimensionless ≥ 0
    aleatoric_var = blr.b / (blr.a - 1.0) # ≥ 0; aleatoric noise scale (a > 1)
    return mean, epistemic_leverage, aleatoric_var

These three numbers fully characterize the predictive distribution. Integrating out the noise variance gives a Student-t with 2a degrees of freedom, location mᵀφ, and scale (b/a)(1 + φᵀΛ⁻¹φ). In the asymptotic a → ∞ regime that approaches a Gaussian with variance aleatoric_var · (1 + epistemic_leverage). The middle return value is deliberately a dimensionless leverage, not a variance, because mixing units silently is one of the easiest probabilistic mistakes to make.

Density tracker, calibration bins, drift baseline. These three structures don’t enter the forward path directly; they accumulate evidence that drives the structural-decision policy.

  • The density tracker maintains a running diagonal-Gaussian estimate over the leaf’s feature space (Welford mean + Kahan-compensated M2 per dimension). Mahalanobis distance from this distribution is the density score — high values mean “this input is unusual for this leaf.”
  • The calibration bins are a 15-bin reliability diagram: predicted-probability bins are filled with observed outcomes, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) is accumulated with Kahan compensation. A node whose ECE stays below a threshold across three consecutive observation windows is considered calibration-stable.
  • The drift baseline is a frozen snapshot of the density tracker captured when the leaf becomes mature. The L2 z-shift between the current density and the baseline is the drift score — large drift can trigger auto-unfreeze.

The three combine into a composite ood_score = max(density_score, prefix_distance, epistemic_z). This composite is an engineering heuristic (if any of three independent OOD-suggestive signals fires, abstain), not a probabilistic anomaly score with a clean posterior interpretation. It’s defensible because each component is itself well-grounded, but readers expecting a fully Bayesian abstain criterion should treat this as reasonable extrapolation, not established research.

Structural adaptation: decide_step

So far the graph is fixed. The interesting move is letting it change.

ABNG defines six structural actions:

Action Effect
Grow Add a new child leaf at an unbound route-key byte.
Split Take a leaf with sufficient evidence and partition it into two children.
Merge Combine two sibling leaves into one (NIG-aware: precisions sum, means precision-weighted, IG params combined).
Prune Mark a low-evidence leaf as inactive.
Compress Collapse a chain of equivalent sub-trees into a Dense node.
Freeze Stop updating a mature, calibrated leaf to protect it from drift.

Each action is gated on a deterministic condition computed from per-node statistics. The engine that fires them is decide_step:

def decide_step(graph, policy):
    # SNAPSHOT INVARIANT: iterate over node ids captured at call entry.
    # Actions inside this loop append new nodes / mark existing ones inactive,
    # but they do NOT change the targets of this iteration.
    snapshot = sorted(graph.nodes.keys())
    for node_id in snapshot:
        node = graph.nodes[node_id]
        if not node.is_active: continue

        # 1. Drift-trip auto-unfreeze BEFORE the action ladder
        if node.is_frozen and drift_score(node) > policy.drift_unfreeze:
            unfreeze(graph, node_id)
            continue

        # 2. Fixed fall-through order — at most ONE action per node per call
        if try_compress(graph, node_id, policy): continue
        if try_merge(graph, node_id, policy):    continue
        if try_split(graph, node_id, policy):    continue
        if try_prune(graph, node_id, policy):    continue
        if try_grow(graph, node_id, policy):     continue
        try_freeze(graph, node_id, policy)

Three things are worth emphasizing:

  1. Determinism is structural. The node iteration order is NodeId ascending. The action order is fixed. The triggers are pure functions of the graph state and the policy thresholds. Two runs with the same seed and the same observation stream produce the same decide_step decisions, byte-for-byte.

  2. At most one action per node per call. This is what makes the engine analyzable. If Compress and Split both fire on the same node, Compress wins because it comes first in the fall-through; Split waits for the next decide_step call.

  3. The snapshot-at-call-entry invariant. Actions taken inside decide_step (creating new leaves, marking existing ones inactive) do not feed back into that same call’s iteration set. Without this, a single call could chain-fire actions across newly-created descendants — non-deterministic, hard to reason about, and impossible to replay. The snapshot is the simplest guarantee that the call’s effect is bounded.

Gates are not just thresholds. Phase 0.4 sharpened the triggers from “samples seen ≥ N” to genuine quality gates:

  • Merge requires both Hamming-similar signatures and KL-divergence between posteriors below kl_merge.
  • Split requires both sample-count and a deterministic bootstrap held-out ΔNLL gain ≥ nll_split_gain (with SplitMix64 seeding the bootstrap).
  • Grow requires both sample-count and route-key entropy at the candidate depth > H_grow.

The audit chain

Everything that mutates state appends one event. The event has a payload (specific to the kind) and a new_hash computed by SHA-256-chaining over the previous event’s hash:

new_hash = sha256(prev_hash || canonical_bytes(payload))

Thirty audit kinds are defined (tags 0x00 through 0x1D), covering everything from Created (graph genesis) and NodeAdded through BlrUpdated, CalibrationUpdated, Split, Merge, Unfreeze, down to opt-in StatsSnapshot (log-compaction marker, 0x1A), Routed (per-descent trace, 0x1B), ProvenanceStamped (per-node lineage commitment, Phase 0.5, 0x1C), and BeliefUpdateBatch (Phase 0.6 batched observe, 0x1D).

Figure 5 — The audit chain. Each event’s new_hash is the SHA-256 of prev_hash || canonical_payload(event). The chain head is the most recent new_hash — a single 32-byte commitment to the entire training history.

Why this matters: given a serialized snapshot, you can call abng_replay and get back a graph whose chain_head matches the stored final_hash, bit-for-bit. If anything in the log or any per-node state has been tampered with, the replay fails with a typed error (ChainMismatch, BlrStateHashMismatch, …).

The audit chain has one subtle property worth knowing: frequently-updated state (BLR posteriors, calibration bins, density trackers) emit witness-only events — the event carries just the new state hash, not the full state — because the actual blob lives in the snapshot’s per-node section. This keeps event size bounded under heavy training; you don’t pay 100 MB of audit log per epoch.

Potential Failure Mode — audit log size

Even with witness-only events at ~80 bytes apiece, four state updates per observation across 1 M training rows is ~300 MB of audit log. That fits on disk but is painful in memory. Phase 0.7’s abng_compact_log builtin and the StatsSnapshot event let users squash long runs of *Updated events into a single snapshot marker; Phase 0.8 is considering Merkle-indexed chains for sub-linear verification cost. Without compaction, audit-log size is the most likely first scaling pain point a real user would hit.

Temporal evolution

The trie isn’t static even at fixed graph size: per-node state evolves with every observation. A leaf’s BLR posterior tightens, its density tracker accumulates mass, its calibration bins fill. Three time-aware pieces of state are worth calling out explicitly:

  • NodeSignature — a 32-byte Welford-smoothed summary across four channels (prediction, uncertainty, calibration, routing).
  • Maturity flags — samples_seen, calibration_stable, uncertainty_stable, trust_level. Once uncertainty_stable first holds, the leaf’s expected epistemic variance is auto-captured and becomes the denominator in the calibrated OOD ratio.
  • 3-window stability ring buffers — three consecutive observation windows of ECE and σ_epi. A leaf only counts as calibration-stable when max(ece_history) − min(ece_history) stays under a configured tolerance across all three windows.

The combination of these is what lets decide_step decide, deterministically, when a leaf has earned an action like Freeze or Split.

Determinism: what it costs, what it buys

Everything above runs under a strict determinism contract inherited from CJC-Lang:

  • Summation: all f64 sums use KahanAccumulatorF64 or pairwise_sum_f64. Plain += reductions are banned in canonical paths.
  • Hashing: SHA-256 from cjc_snap::hash::sha256, a hand-rolled FIPS 180-4 implementation. Zero external dependencies.
  • Random numbers: cjc_repro::Rng, a SplitMix64 PRNG, seeded explicitly from (graph.seed, node_id, …) derivations.
  • Maps and sets: BTreeMap and BTreeSet only. HashMap is banned in canonical paths because its iteration order is randomized.
  • Float canonical bytes: f64::to_bits().to_be_bytes() everywhere — preserves NaN bit patterns and is platform-stable.
  • No FMA in belief-touching kernels. Fused multiply-add can give slightly different bits than a*b + c on some hardware; ABNG avoids it for the BLR Cholesky, the Welford updates, and the calibration ECE accumulation.

The cost is performance: ABNG’s MLP forward path is slower than vendor-tuned BLAS would be. Phase 0.7 has been chipping away at this with caller-provided buffers (Item B route encoding: 2.37× speedup; Item C streaming SHA-256: 1.18×; Item F stack-array chain advance: 1.24×; the fused abng_train_step builtin: 1.17×). Cumulatively, around 4–5 µs saved per training row on a representative workload. It is not yet competitive with a hand-tuned PyTorch loop on GPU — that comparison would not be honest to make.

The benefit is what the rest of this article keeps coming back to: same seed, same data, byte-identical model snapshot, across re-runs and (currently) across x86-64 Linux / macOS / Windows.

Putting it together

The full lifecycle of one observation (x, y) looks like this:

def observe_and_update(graph, x, y, policy):
    # 1. Route
    route_key = graph.codebook.encode(x)
    leaf = descend(graph, route_key).leaf
    if graph.opt_in_routed_audit:
        audit_append(graph, Routed(leaf.id, matched_prefix))

    # 2. Forward through MLP body
    phi = leaf.mlp.forward(x)              # learned features

    # 3. Predict (consumed by user, not state-changing)
    mean, epi, ale = blr_predict(leaf.blr, phi)
    ood = composite_ood(leaf, phi, epi)

    # 4. Update belief state with the new (phi, y)
    leaf.blr.update(phi, y)                # closed-form NIG step
    leaf.density.observe(phi)              # Welford diag
    leaf.calibration.observe(prob_of(mean, ale), y)
    audit_append(graph, BlrUpdated(leaf.id, leaf.blr.state_hash()))
    audit_append(graph, DensityUpdated(leaf.id, leaf.density.state_hash()))
    audit_append(graph, CalibrationUpdated(leaf.id, leaf.calibration.state_hash()))

    # 5. Update node signature + maturity
    leaf.signature.welford_update(prediction=mean, uncertainty=epi, ...)
    leaf.maturity.recompute()

    # 6. Periodically: structural adaptation
    if graph.step_counter % policy.decide_every == 0:
        decide_step(graph, policy)

Every line in this routine maps to a real abng_* builtin in cjc-runtime and cjc-abng/src/dispatch.rs — observable from CJC-Lang source, replayable from the audit log, byte-identical across re-runs.

Key Takeaways — Section 3
  • Routing is discrete and traceable: a learned codebook converts features to bytes; the trie descends one byte at a time.
  • Each leaf is a small Bayesian model: MLP body + Bayesian Linear Regression head with closed-form Normal-Inverse-Gamma updates. The predictive distribution is a Student-t; epistemic leverage and aleatoric scale are surfaced separately so the user can never accidentally mix units.
  • decide_step is the structural engine: deterministic order, at most one action per node per call, gated by quality conditions (KL, ΔNLL, route entropy). The snapshot-at-call-entry invariant bounds each call’s effect.
  • Everything appends an audit event. The chain-head is a single SHA-256 commitment to the entire training history; the most likely scaling pain point in practice is audit-log size, which compaction addresses.
  • Determinism is a hard constraint, not a best-effort property. Kahan summation, SplitMix64, BTreeMap, no FMA — for every state mutation.

The composition has a deliberate property: the predictive distribution and the OOD score are computed from overlapping but non-identical signals. This is by design — it catches cases where the BLR has over-extrapolated with confidence, and it makes the composite deliberately over-conservative: false positives on OOD are accepted in exchange for fewer false negatives.

Data-driven visualizations users should actually plot

Once you have a running ABNG model, five plots come up repeatedly as diagnostics:

1. Sankey of routing flow. For a held-out batch, a Sankey diagram with each band-thickness proportional to observations through that route prefix. Reveals bottlenecks (a single leaf absorbing >40% of inputs may need Split), underused leaves (Prune candidates), cold paths (zero-input branches), and drift (when the route population shifts across time-slices). Source data: opt-in Routed audit events (0x1B).

2. Per-node activation and uncertainty heatmap. A 2-D matrix with one row per leaf and one column per metric (samples_seen, ECE, epi_leverage, aleatoric_var, drift_score, is_frozen). Hot rows (high ECE + high leverage) are under-trained leaves; cold rows are well-calibrated frozen leaves; drift outliers are about to auto-unfreeze.

3. 2-D uncertainty field over an input slice. For scientific applications, grid-evaluate the model on a 100×100 mesh and plot three side-by-side fields: epistemic leverage, aleatoric scale, composite OOD score. A well-trained ABNG model produces a piecewise-smooth uncertainty surface — with sharp ridges at codebook bin edges. Those ridges are an artefact of the discrete codebook, not of the underlying physics; the tradeoff against smoother soft-router architectures is explicit and not removable without redesigning the audit story.

4. Single-leaf state-transition graph. Filter the audit log to a specific leaf id and render its action history: Born → Maturing → Mature → Frozen → [drift trip] → Unfrozen → …. This is the closest thing ABNG has to “explaining what happened to this part of the network” and is usually the first thing to look at when debugging a specific failure.

5. Cumulative structural action counts. A stacked area chart of Grow / Split / Merge / Prune / Compress / Freeze events over training time. Healthy dynamics show an early burst of Grow, late Freezes, occasional Splits at distribution boundaries. Instability shows alternating Merges and Grows (structural thrashing — usually a sign that kl_merge or H_grow is mis-tuned).

Benchmark plot suggestions

ABNG has no published benchmarks yet. The honest move is to specify exactly what plots would confirm or refute the design hypothesis, so a reader can hold the project accountable.

1. Uncertainty calibration plot (reliability diagram). For a held-out test set, bin predictions by predicted probability (15 bins, matching ABNG’s internal ECE) and plot observed frequency vs. predicted probability per bin. Pass criterion: weighted ECE across leaves < 0.05 on well-specified tasks (synthetic NIG regression, calibrated classifiers).

2. Sparse routing efficiency. Plot n_leaves_touched / n_leaves_total over time. Pass criterion: routing fraction stays approximately O(1).

3. Determinism verification. Train two graphs with the same seed and the same data; plot the byte-level diff of their final snapshots as a function of training steps. Pass criterion: diff is identically zero at every step.

4. Memory efficiency vs. log compaction. Plot audit-log size in MB vs. training steps, with and without abng_compact_log enabled. Pass criterion: compacted log grows sub-linearly.

5. Convergence of per-leaf posteriors on synthetic NIG. For a synthetic task where the true (weights, σ²) are known, plot ‖m_t − w_true‖ and b_t/(a_t − 1) − σ²_true over training steps. Pass criterion: both decay to numerical noise; b/(a−1) should approach σ²_true from above.

6. Comparison against a deterministic-but-rigid baseline. For the same dataset, compare ABNG against a fixed-depth decision tree with Bayesian leaf models. Pass criterion: ABNG ≥ tree on calibration ECE; ABNG node count tracks data complexity in a way the static tree cannot.

7. Error-vs-OOD-score calibration (critical for scientific ML). On held-out OOD test points, bin by predicted ood_score and plot mean test error in each bin. Pass criterion: monotonic — the more OOD-flagged a prediction, the worse the average accuracy. This is the plot that says “abstain when ABNG says abstain” is a defensible policy.

These seven aren’t the only benchmarks. They are the seven that, once produced, would let a reader form a defensible opinion on whether the architecture works.

What ABNG might actually be good for — and what it would cost

The architecture described above is not yet validated. This section covers the plausible advantages — the cases where the design choices should pay off — but every advantage is paired with its caveat. If a reader walks away thinking “this clearly wins everywhere,” I have written it wrong.

Each subsection follows the same shape: Claim → Mechanism → Tradeoff → Honest tag (Established, Extrapolation, or Speculative).

Auditability of training history

Claim. Given an ABNG model snapshot and its audit log, the entire training history is recoverable bit-for-bit, and any tampering anywhere in that history surfaces as a typed error.

Mechanism. SHA-256 hash-chained audit log over every state mutation. abng_replay walks the chain from genesis, verifying each event’s new_hash against the recomputed chain head and each per-node blob against its most recent *Initialized / *Updated witness.

Tradeoff. The audit chain commits to state mutations, not to model decisions. It tells you the exact sequence of (observation → update → structural action) that produced the model, but it does not tell you why a leaf made a specific prediction in a LIME/SHAP sense. Audit log size grows linearly in observations unless compaction is enabled; even with witness-only events, a 1 M-row training run is ~300 MB on disk. SHA-256 is tamper-evident, not tamper-proof — an attacker who controls the entire training pipeline can produce a self-consistent malicious log. The right mental model is a git commit history for a model: complete, replayable, cryptographically committed, but only as trustworthy as the entity producing the events.

Tag. Established — Merkle / hash-chain audit logs are well-understood in distributed systems and databases. The application to ML training history is unusual but the cryptographic primitive is standard.

Native uncertainty tracking

Claim. Every prediction comes with separately-reported epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty, computed from a closed-form posterior, with no sampling or variational machinery.

Mechanism. Per-leaf Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior over the BLR head; predictive distribution is Student-t with 2a degrees of freedom, location mᵀφ, scale (b/a)(1 + φᵀΛ⁻¹φ). Epistemic leverage is the dimensionless φᵀΛ⁻¹φ; aleatoric scale is b/(a−1) in units of y²; both surface as separate return values to prevent silent unit mixing.

Tradeoff. The uncertainty is local — it is the posterior of the BLR conditional on the routing decision. A clean global predictive distribution would require integrating over routing uncertainty, which ABNG approximates with the composite ood_score heuristic but does not perform exactly. The BLR posterior also assumes Gaussian noise and linear features (after the MLP body); model-misspecification effects show up as a systematically inflated b term. The inflation is asymmetric — b can grow arbitrarily large under bad fit but cannot shrink below its prior. Calibration depends on enough samples per leaf: sparsely-routed leaves stay broadly uncertain regardless of the global model’s quality.

Tag. Established at the local-BLR level. Reasonable extrapolation for the graph-level predictive interpretation.

Suitability for scientific machine learning

Claim. ABNG’s combination of calibrated uncertainty, replayability, and structural adaptation makes it a candidate for physics-informed networks, surrogate solvers, and inverse problems — domains where confidently wrong outputs corrupt downstream computation.

Mechanism. Per-prediction posterior tells the simulation when to fall back to the high-fidelity model or to gather more data. Replayable audit log lets the simulation author reproduce a specific belief state at a specific moment. Structural adaptation allows the surrogate to allocate capacity to regions of input space where physics demands more resolution. CJC-Lang already runs PINN demos against analytical PDEs via the cjc-ad autodiff machinery — the 1D heat equation, Burgers, Allen-Cahn, KdV, and wave equations all have parity-tested implementations in the workspace.

Tradeoff. ABNG is not yet competitive with Fourier Neural Operators or DeepONet on operator-learning benchmarks. The discrete codebook + trie routing is not discretization-invariant in the way FNO is; predictions are piecewise smooth across leaves, with discontinuities at codebook bin edges. This is a fundamental limitation, not a fixable bug — discretization invariance would require dropping the codebook entirely, which would force redesigning the audit story (you cannot byte-trace a soft routing decision the same way). The per-leaf MLP body is small (typically 8–64 hidden units); global expressivity is limited even with many leaves. For a benchmark like 2D Darcy flow, ABNG should be expected to underperform a tuned FNO and overperform a tuned random forest, on a roughly intermediate compute budget.

Tag. Reasonable extrapolation for the use case; speculative on actual numerical competitiveness until benchmarks land.

Reproducible simulation embedding

Claim. ABNG models can be embedded in Monte Carlo or sensitivity-analysis loops and the entire pipeline — model + simulation — produces bit-identical output across re-runs.

Mechanism. Deterministic execution contract inherited from CJC-Lang: Kahan summation, SplitMix64 RNG threaded explicitly, no FMA in canonical paths, BTreeMap throughout. The simulation author seeds the model and the sampler from the same root seed; the model’s contribution is a pure function of (seed, observations).

Tradeoff. Determinism does not make a simulation correct, only repeatable. A bug in either the model or the sampler is reproduced exactly the same way every time. Determinism is also currently single-threaded — parallel Monte Carlo replicas require multiple seeded sub-RNGs, not a rayon::par_iter over a shared model. There is no GPU offload, so wall-clock time per replica is bounded by CPU performance.

Tag. Established at the language-substrate level. Reasonable extrapolation for the end-to-end embedding.

Tradeoff Snapshot — first half of the eight advantages
  • Auditability is replay, not explanation.
  • Native uncertainty is local, not global.
  • Scientific ML fit is intermediate, not state-of-the-art.
  • Reproducibility is bit-equality, not correctness.

Each property is real and shipped. Each one has a corner the design cannot reach without giving up another property. The combination is the bet; no single axis is.

Probabilistic reasoning at the routing level

Claim. Because routing is discrete and traceable, you can ask probabilistic questions about the routing decision itself — “what fraction of inputs route to this leaf?”, “what’s the entropy of the route distribution at depth 3?” — and get exact answers from the audit log.

Mechanism. Opt-in Routed audit events (0x1B) record each (leaf_id, matched_prefix). Aggregations over the log give exact marginal route distributions, conditional distributions, and entropies at any depth. The structural decision policy already uses route entropy at the candidate-depth gate for Grow — this is the same machinery exposed to user code.

Tradeoff. The Routed events are opt-in and off by default, because per-descent logging at high observation rates can dwarf the model’s actual training data. The probabilistic questions answerable from the log are also structural (about routing), not predictive (about model outputs) — the latter still requires running the model.

Tag. Reasonable extrapolation — the mechanism is real and shipped, but its use as a routing-analysis tool is so far an internal-debugging affordance.

Adaptive memory through structural decisions

Claim. The graph grows when it needs capacity, splits when a leaf becomes heterogeneous, merges when two leaves become equivalent, prunes when a leaf is unused, freezes when a leaf is mature, and unfreezes when drift demands it.

Mechanism. decide_step engine with the six structural actions described earlier, gated by quality conditions (KL divergence for Merge, bootstrap ΔNLL for Split, route entropy for Grow). Drift baseline + drift_unfreeze threshold automatically re-enables a frozen leaf when its input distribution shifts.

Tradeoff. The deterministic firing of structural actions is guaranteed; the quality of those actions depends on the policy thresholds, which require tuning per dataset. Mis-tuned thresholds produce visible failure modes: too-tight kl_merge prevents legitimate consolidation, too-loose H_grow causes runaway proliferation. There is no default policy that works across radically different data distributions, and there is currently no automatic threshold-tuning beyond hand-rolled grid search. TOML preset templates for common scenarios are planned for a future phase rather than shipped today.

Tag. Reasonable extrapolation — the mechanism works in unit tests and small-scale demos; performance at production scale is speculative.

Sparse computation at inference

Claim. Each prediction touches exactly one leaf, regardless of how large the graph has grown. Inference cost is O(d_route) for the trie descent + O(d_feat²) for the BLR predict, independent of total node count.

Mechanism. ART-style children layout means trie descent is one byte-indexed lookup per level; route key length is bounded by the configured codebook depth. The BLR Cholesky pre-solve is reused across predictions on the same leaf; predict-time work is a single dot product plus a triangular solve.

Tradeoff. Inference is sparse, but training is not as sparse as it looks. The whole graph’s structural-decision policy runs on decide_step, which is O(n_active_nodes) — sub-millisecond at 10 K nodes, still under a second at 1 M. The audit chain advance is global — every observation appends an event, and verify_chain is O(n_events) at roughly 100–200 ns/event after Phase 0.7’s streaming-hash optimization (so verifying a 1 M-event chain is ~150 ms). The cost model is: cheap per inference (sub-microsecond), moderate per decide_step call (sub-millisecond), bounded but non-trivial on full audit verification (linear in event count).

Tag. Established at the per-inference level; reasonable extrapolation for the cost model at full training scale.

Deterministic execution as a first-class property

Claim. Two ABNG runs with the same seed and the same observations produce a byte-identical model snapshot, verifiable by comparing 32-byte SHA-256 chain heads.

Mechanism. Determinism contract enforced at the substrate level: Kahan-compensated summation, no FMA in belief-touching kernels, SplitMix64 explicit-seeded RNG, BTreeMap iteration order, f64::to_bits() canonical encoding, hand-rolled SHA-256.

Tradeoff. Pay-as-you-go: every choice that buys determinism costs performance. Kahan accumulation is ~2× the FLOPs of plain summation. Disallowing FMA gives up ~10–30% on modern CPUs depending on workload. BTreeMap is 2–5× slower than HashMap on most lookups. Cumulatively, ABNG cannot currently match the wall-clock performance of a hand-tuned PyTorch + cuDNN pipeline; the determinism premium is real and not negligible. Determinism also restricts ABNG to CPU, single-threaded, x86-64 platforms tested (Linux/macOS/Windows); GPU offload, multi-threading inside one graph, and ARM verification are deferred.

Tag. Established at the substrate level. Reasonable extrapolation for the broader practical implications.

What could go wrong

This is the most important section in the article. Everything before it has been describing what ABNG is and what it might do well. This section describes what could break, what the design cannot fix, and where the architecture is most likely to fail when stressed. If you’re considering using ABNG for anything serious, read this section more carefully than any other.

Each subsection follows: Failure mode → Mechanism that causes it → Whether there’s a known recovery → Severity.

Routing instability at codebook bin edges

Failure. Two inputs x = 0.499 and x = 0.501 may quantize into different codebook bins and therefore route to different leaves. The prediction surface has discontinuities at every bin edge — a small input perturbation can produce an arbitrarily different output.

Cause. The codebook is discrete and frozen after calibration. Bin edges are quantile-based, so they are placed where the training distribution had mass, but two inputs straddling an edge are routed independently regardless of how close they are in feature space.

Recovery. None inherent to the architecture. The mitigations are:

  • Wider codebook bins (fewer leaves total) — reduces edge frequency but loses resolution.
  • Output smoothing post-hoc — averaging predictions across neighbouring leaves, but breaks the audit story (which prediction came from which leaf?).
  • Soft routing — would give up determinism and replayability entirely.

Severity. Fundamental. Any architecture that uses discrete routing pays this cost. ABNG made the explicit tradeoff: discontinuity at bin edges in exchange for traceability and replay. For continuous physics, this is the single biggest reason ABNG will lose to FNO on smoothness benchmarks.

Graph explosion under loose policy thresholds

Failure. If H_grow is set too low, every observation in a slightly novel route triggers a new leaf. The trie proliferates until memory exhausts. In the worst case, an ABNG run on a high-entropy input distribution can produce more nodes than observations.

Cause. Grow requires samples_seen ≥ grow_min and route entropy at the candidate depth > H_grow. Mistune either threshold and the gate opens too easily.

Recovery. Partial. The companion gates (kl_merge, nll_split_gain, drift_unfreeze, freeze threshold) can in principle compensate by consolidating after-the-fact, but they are slower than Grow to fire. A graph that grows to 100K leaves in the first 1K observations is unlikely to recover without a hard restart.

Severity. Tunable. The threshold space is 14-dimensional and currently hand-tuned per dataset. Preset templates (tabular_default, chess_rl, pinn) are planned but not yet shipped. Until automated policy tuning lands, every new dataset is a small research project.

Local calibration without global calibration

Failure. Every leaf is individually well-calibrated by 15-bin ECE, but the graph-level predictions can still be miscalibrated, because routing is non-uniform across leaves and the routing decision is not part of the calibration bins.

Cause. This is a calibration-under-mixture effect — well-studied in the classifier-stacking literature. Each leaf has good per-bin calibration; the aggregate, weighted by routing frequency, can drift in either direction. ABNG’s expected_epistemic auto-capture partially addresses this for OOD detection but does not produce a global calibration estimator.

Recovery. Partial. Computing a graph-level ECE requires aggregating across routed observations, which is straightforward post-hoc. Building it into decide_step as a quality gate is a Phase 0.5+ open item. A user who needs global calibration today can compute it from the audit log, but it isn’t auto-tracked.

Severity. Moderate. For applications where local calibration suffices (per-region uncertainty, OOD detection), this is fine. For global predictive intervals on a whole dataset, this is a real gap.

Convergence is empirical, not proven

Failure. The decide_step engine fires Grow / Split / Merge / Prune / Compress / Freeze in a deterministic order, but there is no published proof that the graph eventually stabilizes on a fixed topology under stationary data.

Cause. The structural actions interact: a recent Split produces children that may later be Merged back together; a Freeze may be reversed by Unfreeze on drift. Under adversarial or distribution-shifting data, the policy could in principle oscillate forever.

Recovery. Empirical, with a plausibility argument. Under stationary data with stable per-node signatures (enforced by the 3-window stability ring buffers), per-leaf BLR posteriors converge by the standard NIG-conjugate result, and the structural-action gates stop firing once signatures stabilize. This is not a proof of graph-topology convergence, but it is a stronger plausibility argument than “it seems to work in tests.” In practice, on the workloads tested (PINN demos, small tabular regressions, the chess-RL retrofit scaffold), the graph stabilizes within a few thousand observations.

Severity. Moderate. For deployed systems this is a real concern — a model that re-restructures itself under operational drift is harder to certify than a static one.

Hardware inefficiency and the GPU mismatch

Failure. ABNG is single-threaded, CPU-only, FMA-disabled, BTreeMap-bound. It cannot saturate modern accelerator hardware. A GPU-equipped workstation gets no measurable speedup over a laptop for ABNG training.

Cause. The determinism contract. Every choice that buys bit-identical replay costs the architecture access to hardware that delivers determinism only weakly (vendor BLAS / cuDNN / FlashAttention all use reduction orderings that are not bit-stable).

Recovery. None without redesigning the determinism story. A “fast mode” that relaxes determinism could be added (similar to PyTorch’s deterministic=False), but it would defeat the audit-chain claim — bit-identical replay would no longer hold.

Severity. Fundamental. This is the single biggest practical limitation. The architecture is intentionally designed not to chase modern hardware. Users who need GPU throughput will not use ABNG; users who need replayability will pay the cost. There is no path to “both.”

Debugging the haystack

Failure. A specific prediction is wrong. The audit log has 1 million events. Which one is responsible?

Cause. Deterministic replay is both the easiest and hardest thing about debugging ABNG. Easy: any bug is reproducible. Hard: the bug may be 100K events deep in a SHA-256 chain, and the chain is not human-readable. The composite ood_score flags suspicious predictions, but pinpointing which observation corrupted a posterior requires walking the audit log node-by-node and event-by-event.

Recovery. Tooling-dependent. cjcl abng inspect --node N --audit filters the log to a specific node; descend_traced records routing paths for individual predictions; abng_predict_snap captures prediction-time state for explainability. These help, but they don’t automate the diagnosis — they make the manual work tractable.

Severity. Moderate. Most users will treat the audit log as a postmortem artefact rather than a live debugging surface.

The high-dimensional input problem

Failure. As input dimensionality grows past ~32, the route key (one byte per dimension) gets long, routing entropy explodes, and the architecture either grows leaves indefinitely or wastes most of the trie on noise dimensions.

Cause. The codebook is per-dimension and the route is concatenated bytes. There is no native dimensionality reduction; high-D inputs need an upstream encoder (a deep network, an autoencoder, a hashing trick) before they hit ABNG, and that encoder is outside the audit story.

Recovery. Partial via composition. Users can deterministically pre-encode high-D inputs (e.g. PCA, fixed random projection, hashed embedding) and feed the low-D output to ABNG. The audit chain then commits to “model + low-D input + observations” but does not capture the pre-encoder’s parameters unless those are separately hashed.

Severity. Moderate. This restricts ABNG’s natural domain to low-D scientific / tabular workloads. ABNG on raw images, audio, or token sequences is not the design target.

The mixed-type, categorical, and classification gaps

Failure. The QuantileCodebook is real-valued only — categorical features have no native representation. The BLR head is Gaussian-noise linear regression — multi-class classification has no closed-form conjugate analogue.

Cause. NIG conjugacy is specific to the Gaussian likelihood; quantile binning of integer-valued categorical features produces degenerate bins.

Recovery. Planned. Categorical codebook support and a Dirichlet-Categorical head type are on the Phase 0.5+ roadmap. As of today, users must pre-encode categoricals before feeding to ABNG, and classification needs a clunky binary-regression workaround.

Severity. Low-to-moderate. For pure tabular ML with mixed types this is a hard blocker; for scientific ML with real-valued regression it doesn’t apply.

What ABNG borrows, what it resembles, what (if anything) is new

ABNG is a recombination of existing techniques, not a singular invention. This section pairs each component with its closest prior art and classifies the relationship as borrowed wholesale, resembles, or genuinely novel in combination (note: the latter is the strongest claim, and even it is hedged).

Bayesian Graph Neural Networks

Bayesian GNN variants in the ML literature (e.g. Bayesian Graph Convolutional Networks by Zhang, Pal, Coates & Üstebay, AAAI 2019; Hasanzadeh et al., Bayesian Graph Neural Networks with Adaptive Connection Sampling, ICML 2020) place a posterior — typically variational or MCMC — over the GNN’s weights and use the resulting predictive distribution for uncertainty quantification.

ABNG differs in two ways:

  1. ABNG does not put a posterior over network weights. The MLP body in each leaf is point-estimated by gradient descent; only the final linear regression head has a posterior. This is a deliberate choice — variational posteriors over MLP weights are expensive and the mean-field approximation is famously poor; conjugate updates over a linear head are cheap and exact.
  2. ABNG’s graph topology is learned at training time; Bayesian GNNs typically operate on a graph supplied as input. The structural decision policy is the part of ABNG that has no Bayesian-GNN analogue.

Relationship: resembles. Same vocabulary (“Bayesian + graph + uncertainty”), different mechanics.

Probabilistic Graphical Models

Directed (Bayesian networks) and undirected (Markov random fields) PGMs (Koller & Friedman 2009) define a joint distribution over many variables and support inference via message passing, sampling, or variational methods.

ABNG is not a PGM. The trie nodes do not represent random variables in a joint distribution; they represent experts in a mixture model. There is no inter-leaf message passing. The audit chain commits to a sequence of state mutations (closer to a database write-ahead log than a PGM’s belief propagation).

A note on overloaded vocabulary: “belief state” gets used in at least four incompatible senses across the literature — (a) the posterior over latent variables in a PGM, (b) a sufficient statistic for prediction in control theory, (c) a state estimator in a POMDP, or (d) the Bayesian posterior of a model conditional on data. ABNG uses (d) — specifically, the BLR posterior at the routed leaf. The shared word “belief” across these senses is genuinely misleading.

Relationship: resembles only in vocabulary.

Mixture-of-Experts Routing

The MoE family (Shazeer et al. 2017 Sparsely-Gated MoE; Lepikhin et al. 2020 GShard; Fedus et al. 2022 Switch Transformer) routes tokens through a fixed population of expert sub-networks via a learned soft gating function, typically trained with auxiliary load-balancing losses.

ABNG’s router differs on three axes:

  1. Discrete, not soft. The route is a byte sequence, not a softmax over experts. This makes routing traceable and gives up some learnability.
  2. Deterministic, not learned. The router has no parameters of its own — the codebook is calibrated once and frozen.
  3. Adaptive population, not fixed. The number of experts changes at training time through Grow / Split / Merge / Prune / Compress.

The older Mixture-of-Trees literature (Bishop & Svensén 2003) and the Bayesian Committee Machines of Tresp (2000) are closer ancestors than modern MoE. A Bayesian Committee Machine is exactly “a mixture of Bayesian Linear Regression experts with a learned partition” — which is most of what ABNG is. Tresp’s original BCM is symmetric — every expert votes with equal access. ABNG is asymmetric — exactly one expert per input, chosen by the trie router. The closer modern relative is Bishop & Svensén’s “product of local experts” with hard routing.

The committee-machine literature lacked the adaptive partitioning, the byte-route trie, and the audit chain, but the core idea of “local Bayesian experts combined under a partition” is theirs.

Relationship to committee machines: direct descendant. This is the closest single prior work.

Neural State-Space Models

S4, S5, Mamba, and the broader linear/structured state-space family (Gu et al. 2021–22; Gu & Dao 2023) compress a sequence into a fixed-size recurrent state and have demonstrated strong long-context performance.

ABNG does not do this. There is no recurrent hidden state. Temporal evolution lives in the per-leaf BLR posterior update, not in a state-space recurrence. ABNG processes observations as i.i.d. samples conditional on routing; the architecture is poorly matched to long-context sequence modelling.

Relationship: unrelated.

Liquid Neural Networks

Hasani et al. 2020 Liquid Time-Constant Networks; Hasani et al. 2022 Liquid-S4. Continuous-time dynamics, very small parameter counts, online adaptivity.

ABNG shares the “small per-node footprint and online adaptation” instinct but uses a discrete trie of Bayesian experts rather than continuous-time ODE neurons. Liquid networks’ interpretability comes from low-dimensional dynamics; ABNG’s interpretability comes from traceable routing + audit chain. Different routes to similar destinations.

Relationship: resembles in motivation, unrelated in mechanics.

Active Inference and Predictive Processing

Friston’s free-energy framework (Friston 2010; Parr & Friston 2019) treats perception, action, and learning as minimization of variational free energy. Models actively select observations to reduce uncertainty.

ABNG has a hint of this in two places:

  • The drift-trip auto-unfreeze responds to prediction error by re-enabling learning.
  • The composite OOD score could drive active learning (route under-confident inputs to a teacher) but does not currently do so.

But ABNG is a passive learner: it does not select what to observe. The formal connection to active inference is weak.

Relationship: resembles in flavor.

Adaptive Radix Trees

Leis, Kemper & Neumann 2013 The Adaptive Radix Tree. The ART data structure introduced the Node4 / Node16 / Node48 / Node256 children layout with insert-time promotion for fast in-memory database key lookup.

ABNG borrows ART wholesale. The children-layout enum is mechanically identical; only the payload changes. This is the cleanest borrowing in the entire architecture — ABNG does not modify ART, it reuses it.

Relationship: borrowed wholesale.

Stream-Mining Drift Detection

ADWIN (Bifet & Gavaldà 2007), KSWIN, Page-Hinkley, and related drift detectors track distribution shift in streaming data.

ABNG’s drift baseline is a simplified version of these: an L2 z-shift of the per-leaf density tracker’s mean. The threshold-based auto-unfreeze trigger is reminiscent of stream-mining drift triggers. ABNG’s approach is cruder than ADWIN’s window-based statistical test, but the determinism contract makes a window-based test substantively harder to implement bit-stably.

Relationship: resembles.

Cryptographic Audit Logs

Merkle trees (Merkle 1979), git’s commit DAG, and the broader literature on append-only verifiable logs (CONIKS, Certificate Transparency) provide the cryptographic primitive ABNG uses for replay.

ABNG applies this primitive to ML training history. Current ML lineage tools (MLflow, DVC, Weights & Biases) record snapshots and event metadata, not cryptographically-chained state mutations. ABNG’s granularity — every parameter update has a hash witness — is the unusual choice.

Relationship: borrowed primitive, unusual application.

What, if anything, is genuinely novel

Honestly, no single mechanism. The combination is unusual:

  1. Adaptive trie routing using byte-encoded features (borrowed from ART, applied to ML).
  2. Per-leaf NIG-conjugate Bayesian Linear Regression head (textbook Bayesian linear regression, applied per-leaf in a routed mixture — Bayesian Committee Machine genealogy).
  3. Deterministic decide_step policy engine with six structural actions gated by quality conditions (resembles stream-mining drift detection + decision-tree splitting heuristics, but the specific six-action set with deterministic fall-through is, as far as I can tell, original to ABNG).
  4. SHA-256 audit chain over every state mutation with abng_replay byte-identical verification (borrowed primitive, unusual granularity).
  5. Bit-identical reproducibility contract enforced at the substrate level (inherited from CJC-Lang, not unique to ABNG, but unusual in ML).

If I had to name one “novel in combination” contribution, it would be the integration of these five into a coherent architecture. The Bayesian Committee Machine literature has (2); database research has (4); ART has (1)’s data structure; stream-mining has a piece of (3). No prior work I’m aware of combines all five.

This is a recombination claim, not a novelty claim. The honest framing: ABNG is a careful integration of well-understood components in a direction nobody has yet tried in this exact configuration.

What Makes This Different?

Most architectures borrow one or two ideas and add one new mechanism. ABNG borrows five ideas (none new) and combines them in a way none of the source literatures attempted. The bet is that the integration produces emergent properties — replayability + uncertainty + adaptation in one package — that no individual component delivers alone.

Where ABNG might actually be useful — and where existing tools would still win

This section is structured as application/competitor pairs: for each candidate use case, why ABNG might help, and why an existing tool may still beat it. The pattern is deliberate — every “ABNG could help here” should make you ask “but what would I use today?” The answer is rarely “nothing.”

Scientific computing and PDE surrogates

Why ABNG could help. A neural surrogate for a PDE solver needs calibrated uncertainty to know when to fall back to the high-fidelity solver. The audit chain lets simulation authors reproduce a specific belief state at a specific moment, which matters when scientific results need to be defended. Structural adaptation allocates capacity where the physics demands resolution — refining a leaf in a high-curvature region.

Why existing systems may still win. Fourier Neural Operators (FNO) are discretization-invariant — train at 64×64, evaluate at 256×256, get a consistent solution. ABNG’s discrete codebook routing cannot match this; predictions are piecewise smooth across leaves with discontinuities at bin edges. For smooth-PDE benchmarks like 2D Darcy or 1D Burgers, FNO will outperform ABNG on accuracy at equal compute. ABNG’s pitch is uncertainty + replay, not raw accuracy. One caveat: FNO’s spectral basis suffers Gibbs phenomena at sharp discontinuities (shocks, phase fronts). ABNG’s piecewise routing isn’t elegant but isn’t worse than FNO on discontinuous PDEs. Both architectures are bad in different ways; neither has a clean win on shock-heavy problems.

Honest hypothesis: ABNG fits cases where the simulation author cares more about defending a result than maximizing accuracy. PINN-style use cases with small parameter budgets and per-region uncertainty needs.

Probabilistic time-series forecasting

Why ABNG could help. Per-leaf calibrated intervals on regime-specific forecasts. Routing decisions can encode regime detection (one leaf per regime), and drift-trip unfreeze responds when a regime changes.

Why existing systems may still win. State-space models (Mamba, S5) handle long-context forecasting natively; classical models (ARIMA, exponential smoothing) are well-understood and cheap. Conformal-prediction wrappers on top of either give calibrated intervals without ABNG’s architectural overhead.

Honest hypothesis: ABNG would compete on non-stationary time series with detectable regime changes and a need for per-regime calibration. Stationary or smoothly-varying time series favour the established tools.

Uncertainty-aware reinforcement learning

Why ABNG could help. A value function or Q-network with calibrated uncertainty can drive exploration (act under uncertainty, observe more) and safety (abstain when out-of-distribution). The deterministic replay lets you debug policy failures by replaying the exact training history that led to a bad decision.

Why existing systems may still win. Bootstrapped ensembles, Bayesian DQN variants, and dropout-as-uncertainty all already do per-step exploration with uncertainty. PPO with intrinsic motivation handles exploration without explicit Bayesian machinery. ABNG’s structural overhead is hard to justify when ensemble methods are already well-tuned.

Honest hypothesis: ABNG is most interesting for RL when the audit story matters — regulated agents, safety-critical robotics, RL in clinical decision support. For game-playing RL it’s almost certainly the wrong tool.

Robotics and embedded control

Why ABNG could help. Liquid Networks have demonstrated that small models (a few hundred parameters) suffice for many control tasks. ABNG’s per-leaf models can be similarly small. Per-prediction OOD score gives a controller a principled “I don’t know — fall back to a safe policy” signal. Audit replay makes postmortems of robotic failures tractable.

Why existing systems may still win. Liquid Networks already deliver compact controllers with smooth dynamics; they’re better at the continuous-time aspect of control. Classical Kalman filtering + model-predictive control is mature and well-supported. ABNG is not yet on embedded hardware (CJC-Lang has no embedded story).

Honest hypothesis: ABNG could fit post-deployment robotic diagnostics — replay the audit log of a failed mission and identify which observation degraded which leaf’s posterior. As an online controller, it is not ready.

Anomaly detection

Why ABNG could help. The composite ood_score is literally an anomaly score. Per-leaf density tracking + epistemic leverage + route prefix distance produce three independent flags, max-aggregated. Audit log supports forensic investigation of how the model handled anomalies historically.

Why existing systems may still win. Isolation Forests, deep SVDD, autoencoder reconstruction error, and conformal anomaly detection are all well-studied and have years of tuning. ABNG’s setup cost (codebook calibration + initial training + policy threshold tuning) is high for a single-purpose anomaly detector.

Honest hypothesis: ABNG makes sense for anomaly detection when you also want the predictive head — e.g. “predict X, but also tell me when X is OOD.” Pure anomaly detection without a predictive task should use simpler tools.

Interpretable / auditable ML

Why ABNG could help. Per-leaf inspection (cjcl abng inspect --node N) shows exactly which observations shaped a leaf’s posterior. The audit log is a complete, cryptographically-committed training history. cjcl abng explain <prediction.snap> walks the routing path and lists abstain reasons.

Why existing systems may still win. Random forests with feature importance, gradient boosting with SHAP values, LIME for local explanations, and rule-based models have years of tooling and user familiarity. ABNG’s “audit trail” is different from “explanation” — it tells you what the model learned, not why a specific prediction was that value.

Honest hypothesis: ABNG’s auditability fits regulatory contexts where lineage matters more than prediction-level explanation. For a clinical model facing FDA scrutiny, the audit chain is a defensible artefact in a way SHAP values are not. For ad-targeting interpretability, SHAP wins by a mile.

Scientific data systems and model provenance

Why ABNG could help. ABNG can be a node in a larger data-lineage system. The model’s training history is a hash-chained log; combine that with dataset hashes and pipeline hashes and you get end-to-end cryptographic provenance.

Why existing systems may still win. MLflow, DVC, and W&B already track model lineage at the artefact level (datasets in, models out, with versioned metadata). They don’t track every parameter update, but for most provenance use cases this is fine — and the tooling is mature.

Honest hypothesis: ABNG’s fit is high-stakes provenance — regulated trials, scientific replication packages, model attestation for legal/compliance. For typical ML-ops provenance, the existing tools are appropriate.

Safety-critical forecasting

Why ABNG could help. A forecasting model in a power grid, ICU monitor, or autonomous-vehicle perception stack needs calibrated abstain. ABNG’s per-prediction OOD score + deterministic replay + audit log align well with this use case.

Why existing systems may still win. Established safety-critical ML uses conformal prediction wrappers around well-tested models, formal verification of bounded-output behaviour, and redundant ensemble systems. ABNG would need a long track record before being considered for a safety-critical deployment.

Honest hypothesis: This is a future use case, not a current one. ABNG would need many years of production deployment elsewhere before being trusted here.

The hypothesis is not that ABNG replaces these tools but that it fits cases where multiple of its properties matter simultaneously — and there, today, there is no good competitor because no existing architecture combines those properties.

A research direction, not a result

If you’ve read this far, you have a fairly complete picture of what ABNG is, where it sits in the landscape, what it might do well, what it cannot do, and which existing tools would still beat it on their home turf. None of that constitutes evidence that ABNG works as a deployed system. It is a coherent design under active development, supported by tests and small-scale demos, with no published benchmarks and no production track record.

This conclusion is not “ABNG is good.” It is “ABNG is a falsifiable hypothesis, and here is what falsification would look like.”

What ABNG is, in one sentence again

A routed mixture of Bayesian Linear Regression experts on an adaptive trie, with deterministic execution and a SHA-256 audit chain — built in CJC-Lang, a pre-1.0 research compiler that itself enforces the determinism contract. Both the architecture (Phase 0.7, snapshot v13) and the substrate (CJC-Lang 0.1.x) are under active development.

What would need to happen for this to graduate from “research direction” to “validated architecture”

Four things, in roughly increasing order of difficulty:

1. Cross-platform determinism CI. Today the locked SHA-256 canary hashes are demonstrated on x86-64 Linux, macOS, and Windows in CJC-Lang’s test suite. ARM and BSD are untested. A GitHub Actions matrix running the full ABNG test suite on Linux+ARM, macOS+ARM, and Windows+x64, with canary comparison, is a finite engineering project. Until it’s done, “bit-identical determinism” is a claim with a known footnote.

2. Wall-clock benchmarks at scale. The largest workload ABNG has run is the chess-RL retrofit scaffold (hundreds of observations) and the 1D heat-equation PINN demo (tens of collocation points). The architecture should be benchmarked at n ∈ {10³, 10⁴, 10⁵, 10⁶} against deterministic-but-rigid baselines (Bayesian decision tree, conformalized GP) and against state-of-the-art uncertainty-aware ML (deep ensembles, conformal+transformer). Until those numbers exist, “scales well in theory” is theory.

3. Comparison studies on real benchmarks. The seven benchmark plots specified earlier — reliability diagrams, sparse routing efficiency, determinism diff, log size with compaction, NIG convergence, comparison to a deterministic baseline, error-vs-OOD-score calibration — would constitute a defensible empirical case for the architecture. None of them are technically hard. They are project-management hard: someone has to run them, document the pass criteria, and publish the results.

4. A real-world deployment case study. A practitioner using ABNG for a problem with real stakes — a regulated trial, a scientific replication package, a robotic-system audit — and writing up what worked, what didn’t, and what they would do differently. Until this exists, ABNG is software without a user.

Three specific experiments I want to see run next

If a reader of this article were to pick one experiment to do, this is the order I would suggest:

Experiment A: NIG-convergence sanity check (1–2 weeks). Generate synthetic data from a known (w_true, σ²_true), train ABNG on increasing sample counts, and plot ‖m_t − w_true‖ and b_t/(a_t − 1) − σ²_true. Pass: both should decay to numerical noise, b/(a−1) from above. Fail: either curve plateaus, indicating a posterior misspecification. This is the cheapest experiment that could falsify the architecture’s correctness.

Experiment B: 2D Darcy-flow surrogate comparison (1–2 months). Train ABNG, FNO, and a Bayesian decision tree on the canonical 2D Darcy-flow benchmark. Compare on accuracy (MSE), calibration (ECE), and inference latency. Pass: ABNG is intermediate between FNO and the tree — worse on accuracy than FNO, better on calibration than tree. Fail: ABNG is worst on both axes, which would suggest the architecture’s tradeoffs are not buying what we think they are.

Experiment C: Real-world regulated-ML case study (3–6 months). Find one regulated-ML project (medical, financial, legal) and replicate it with ABNG, comparing audit/replay quality and predictive performance. Pass: the audit chain provides a defensible artefact, predictive performance is within 10% of the production model, and the regulatory team finds the lineage trail useful. Fail: either the predictive performance is uncompetitive, or the regulatory team finds the audit trail no more useful than existing tools.

What this article hopes to do

Not convince anyone that ABNG is the right architecture. Convince a small number of readers that it is a falsifiable, well-specified design worth examining seriously, and worth running one of the experiments above against. If those experiments show the architecture is wrong, that is a useful result. If they show it is partly right, that is a more useful result. Either is better than silence.

If you have a clean idea for an experiment that would either confirm or refute one of the claims in this piece, the project is open source — bring experiments.

The honest summary: ABNG is exploratory engineering, not validated science. I think the design choices add up to something useful for a specific class of problems. I would like to be wrong in specific, instructive ways. This article is the invitation.
