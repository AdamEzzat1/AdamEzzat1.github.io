Made my deterministic programming language "greener" 🌱 — and the most useful thing I learned is that the obvious version of that claim is wrong.

"Determinism = lower energy" is a myth. Stable math (Kahan/binned), audit trails, and bit-exact replay all cost MORE per run, not less.

So I built the honest version — three phases:

🔹 Runtime Policy Layer: one flag (cool / balanced / max-perf) bounds how hard a run drives the CPU, plus a deterministic energy estimate.
🔹 Race-to-idle scheduling: full speed for short bursts, throttle only when load is *sustained* (bursts don't overheat — sustained load does). Clawed back ~1.8× of what a fixed cap costs.
🔹 Fused kernels: one pass, one allocation instead of two — less memory traffic.

What ties it together: thread count, pool size, and the schedule provably never change the output. Same seed → same bits, across every profile and both executors. That's the only reason an adaptive thermal knob is even safe in a language built on reproducibility.

And the real "green" win isn't fewer FLOPs — it's fewer WASTED runs. Unstable systems burn compute on reruns nobody wanted: drift, NaN cascades, failed reproductions. The metric I care about is trustworthy_results / joule — and determinism drives that rerun count toward 1.

And it protects more than today's run: short-term you dodge the throttle cliff (overheating chips clock *down* = slower now); long-term, easing that thermal stress keeps your hardware and performance healthy over time.

One nuance, because it's easy to flip: heat threatens *performance*, not the answer. Throttling changes timing, never the bits — a throttled run is byte-identical to a cool one. The thermal bound protects how fast you go; determinism protects what you get.

Not "greener than everything" — way too early for that. Just exploring whether deterministic, memory-aware, thermally-bounded systems can cut *wasteful* compute where trust matters more than raw speed.
